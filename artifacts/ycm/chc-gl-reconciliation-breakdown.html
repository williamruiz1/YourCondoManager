Cherry Hill Court — Receivables Reconciliation Breakdown

Why the two "what owners owe" figures disagree — and whether the numbers are trustworthy · read-only DB analysis · 2026-07-01

The headline (read this first)

The $6,315 GL gap is small and fully explained — but it is NOT the real problem. The real problem is one level down: ~55 owner payments (~$15,280) landed in the Chase account and only $2,806 of them was ever recorded in the owner ledger. That means the receivable totals themselves are inflated — owners are being shown as owing roughly $12,500 they have already paid into Chase. So BOTH "what owners owe" figures (the $39,797 ledger total AND the $33,482 GL figure) are overstated by the same unapplied-payments amount. The $6,315 gap is a real but minor mirroring artifact sitting on top of a much larger unapplied-payments distortion.

Where the $6,315 comes from — by cause (discriminating test) fully explained · $0 remainder

The GL is a windowed, forward-only mirror of the owner ledger — it is not a continuous feed. Every GL-1200 entry links back to an owner-ledger entry (source_type = 'owner_ledger_entry'). Discriminating test: which owner-ledger entries got mirrored into the GL, and which did not?
  • 57 owner-ledger entries were mirrored into GL-1200 — they sum to exactly $33,481.78 (= the GL figure, to the cent).
  • 36 owner-ledger entries were never mirrored — they sum to exactly $6,315.00 (= the gap, to the cent). Remainder: $0.00.
  • The mirror only covered owner-ledger rows created between May 25 – Jun 2, 2026 (posted May 1 – Jun 1). Anything outside that window never reached the GL.
This confirms hypothesis H1 (forward-only / windowed mirroring), refined. It rules out H2 (no entry-type is systematically excluded — the unmirrored rows are ordinary dues/assessments/payments) and H3 (the ownership reassignments did not cause it — the per-unit gap is a clean uniform $330, not the irregular pattern a reassignment would produce).

The four correctness concerns William raised

The receivables are built almost entirely from charges with barely any payments applied. Composition of the $39,796.78 owner-ledger total:

No duplicate entries were found (checked for same unit + type + amount + date — zero). The charges themselves look sourced correctly (real dues + a one-time May-8 balance import of driveway assessments and dues arrears). The problem is the credit side, not the debit side: only $2,806 of payments is recorded, which is what inflates the receivable — see concern #3.

2 · The May spreadsheet vs July timing  explains part of the gap, not the totals

The spreadsheet William provided is a May 27 2026 snapshot; the live ledger is current through July dues. Reconciling the timing:
  • The $5,940 July HOA dues (the bulk of the $6,315 GL gap) is exactly new activity posted after the May snapshot — it is expected drift, not a true discrepancy. The GL simply hasn't been re-synced since June 1.
  • The May-8 "Imported balance" entries in the ledger ARE the spreadsheet snapshot, loaded once. So the ledger's May baseline and the spreadsheet agree.
  • Timing verdict: most of the GL gap is stale-snapshot drift (June + July dues posted since the mirror last ran), not a data error. But the timing does NOT explain the receivable totals being high — that is the unapplied Chase payments (#3).

Trust verdict: the receivable figures are materially overstated — by roughly $12,500 — because paid money sitting in Chase was never credited. Both totals (ledger $39,797 and GL $33,482) carry this same overstatement. Applying the Chase payments would REDUCE what owners owe by ~$12.5k; it does not change the $6,315 ledger-vs-GL gap (that gap is about mirroring coverage, a separate issue), but it does mean the underlying receivables both systems agree on are too high.

Caveat: ~$15,280 is a conservative classification of individual-name Zelle/deposit inflows; a per-owner match (which deposit belongs to which unit) would refine the exact applicable amount. Internal transfers between the two Chase accounts (~$23,726) and vendor/insurance items were excluded from the owner-payment figure.

4 · The dues rate change ($280 → $330, effective May 2026)  applied correctly

The rate transition was handled correctly and did not cause the gap.
  • Recurring monthly dues are all at $330 from May onward — May, June, July each show 18 units × $330. Correct post-May rate.
  • The pre-May $280 rate is preserved in the imported arrears (3 May-8 "HOA dues arrears" entries at $280) — the ledger carried the old rate for the pre-change periods it imported.
  • The April Zelle payments were $280 (the correct pre-May rate) — but those April payments are the unapplied Chase inflows from #3, not ledger entries.
  • No mischarge found from the rate transition. Owner-months: 3 at $280 (imported arrears), 54 at $330 (recurring May/Jun/Jul), plus 9 legacy $350 assessments from Jan–Mar. The $280→$330 change is not part of the $6,315 gap.

What this means / what to do

Is the ~$6,315 gap trustworthy? Yes — it is real, fully explained, and benign (mostly July dues the GL hasn't mirrored yet). Are the receivable TOTALS trustworthy? No — both are overstated by ~$12.5k of owner payments that hit Chase but were never applied.
  1. Apply the Chase payments first (highest priority). Reconcile the 91 Chase transactions against owner ledgers — the ~55 owner deposits (~$15.3k) need to be credited so owners aren't billed for money they've paid. This is the dominant correctness issue; fix it before trusting any receivable number.
  2. Re-sync / backfill the GL. The GL-1200 mirror is stuck at June 1. Re-running the owner-ledger → GL mirror (including the July dues and the pre-May residue) would close the $6,315 GL gap. Make the mirror continuous, not a one-time window, so it doesn't drift again.
  3. Refresh against a current snapshot, not the May spreadsheet. The May spreadsheet is ~5 weeks stale; June + July dues posted since. Any reconciliation to it must account for that expected drift.
  4. Rate change is clean — no action needed on the $280→$330 transition.
Order of magnitude: the unapplied-payments issue (~$12.5k, owner-facing) dwarfs the GL-mirror gap ($6.3k, internal display). Fix payments application first.
