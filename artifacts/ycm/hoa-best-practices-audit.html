YourCondoManager — HOA Financial Best-Practices Gap Audit

Where YCM's financial engine stands against the established standards for community-association accounting and bank reconciliation — CAI, GAAP for Common Interest Realty Associations (FASB ASC 972 / the AICPA CIRA Audit Guide), and the leading HOA-management platforms. The question this answers: what proven practices are we ignoring, and what does it cost us?

P0 gaps (correctness)
Headline — the foundation is strong. YCM already has a fund-aware double-entry general ledger (operating vs. reserve), a chart of accounts, an idempotent GL posting engine, a confidence-scored multi-signal reconciliation matcher, a learned bank-descriptor→owner alias map, reconciliation periods with open/closed/locked states, a staged delinquency-notice engine, payment plans, collections handoff, and an audit log. This is well ahead of most 18-unit self-managed HOAs. The gaps below are about turning a good subledger into a correct, controlled, GAAP-aligned financial system — and about the one architectural decision the owner already flagged: the ledger is person-centric, not unit-centric.

The three P0 gaps in one breath

1. The balance attaches to a person, not the unit. 2. The real GL is built but switched OFF — the live books are a dues-only subledger with no expense/vendor/bank side. 3. Reconciliation is one-way (matched/unmatched counts), not a true three-way bank-to-book proof, and there's no reserve study driving reserve funding. Everything else compounds off these.

2 · Prioritized gap list — what we're ignoring, ranked by impact

Ranked by impact on correctness and operating efficiency for a real HOA. P0 = correctness/liability risk; P1 = control & compliance; P2 = maturity.

P0-1 Ledger is person-centric, not unit-centric MISSING

Best practice: The assessment receivable is an obligation of the unit. Co-owners are jointly & severally liable; the lien attaches to the property. A per-unit roster of authorized payer names sits beside the unit balance, not as the balance owner.
YCM today: owner_ledger_entries is stamped with both unitId and personId (both required), and the owner statement is scoped to personId. When ownership changes (sale, add/remove co-owner, death), the balance is tied to a person, not the property that legally owes it.
Risk of not doing it: Owner-transitions orphan or mis-attribute balances; joint-and-several liability can't be enforced cleanly; a co-owner's payment may not obviously credit the unit; the reconciliation is forced into name-guessing because "who paid" is the primary key instead of "which unit."
Source: FASB ASC 972 / AICPA CIRA Audit & Accounting Guide; state condominium lien statutes; CAI assessment-collection guidance.
Recommendation: Make the unit the balance-bearing entity. personId becomes optional metadata on a payment (who tendered it), and the unit carries the receivable. Add a per-unit payer roster (authorized names/descriptors) and — critically — issue each unit a stable payment reference / account number so payments resolve to the unit deterministically (see P0-3). This is the architectural pivot the owner already flagged; everything downstream gets simpler.

P0-2 The real books are OFF — live ledger is a single-sided dues subledger PARTIAL

Best practice: Accrual-basis, fund-aware, double-entry GL with a full balance sheet and income/expense statement — the association's real books.
YCM today: A correct, idempotent, fund-aware GL is built (gl_accounts, gl_entries, balanced journals) but gated behind GL_ENABLED (default OFF, per server/services/gl/flag.ts and the schema comments). The live money path writes only owner_ledger_entries — dues in, no expense side, no vendor payables, no cash accounts, no fund tag.
Risk of not doing it: The association has no true financial statements — you can show what owners owe, but not what the HOA spent, owes vendors, or holds by fund. That fails GAAP, blocks the annual audit, and hides the reserve position. Operating and reserve cash are commingled in reality because nothing enforces the split.
Source: FASB ASC 972; AICPA CIRA Guide; CAI financial-reporting best practices.
Recommendation: Drive the GL to source-of-truth for Cherry Hill: run the reconcile-to-the-cent gate (script/verify-gl-reconcile.ts), build the expense/vendor/bank side so journals balance both ways, then flip GL_ENABLED for the association. The subledger becomes a view of the GL, not a parallel truth.

P0-3 No unique payment reference — reconciliation relies on name/amount guessing PARTIAL

Best practice: Every unit gets a unique account/reference number that must appear on the remittance (lockbox coupon, virtual account, or memo reference). Payments resolve to the unit by that key — deterministically — before any name matching is attempted.
YCM today: The matcher is genuinely good for its class — amount + date + fuzzy name + a learned bank_descriptor_aliases map. But the primary signal is still name + amount, which collides (two owners, same dues, same day → both go to manual review by design) and breaks on nicknames, business names, and third-party payers.
Risk of not doing it: Persistent manual-review load, mis-posted payments, and a reconciliation that can't scale past a handful of units. This is the "ad-hoc name-matching" the owner explicitly wants to leave behind.
Source: AppFolio / Alliance Association Bank automated lockbox; Vantaca Pay; Zego lockbox for HOA.
Recommendation: Issue a per-unit reference number (Stripe payment metadata / statement descriptor / lockbox coupon). Match on the reference first (100% confidence, no review), fall back to amount+alias, and keep the fuzzy-name matcher only as the last resort. Pairs directly with P0-1.

P1-1 Reconciliation is one-way, not a three-way balance proof MISSING

Best practice: Monthly three-way reconciliation — bank statement balance = GL cash balance = sum of owner subledger balances, with deposits-in-transit and outstanding items itemized and any difference investigated.
YCM today: report.ts gives matched/unmatched counts — a payment-matching inventory, not a tie-out. There's no assertion that the books actually equal the bank.
Risk: Payments can match individually while the totals silently drift; errors and missing deposits go undetected until an audit.
Source: CAI 10 financial best practices; AICPA CIRA controls; standard bank-rec practice.
Recommendation: Add a period-close tie-out that proves bank = GL cash = subledger, lists reconciling items, and blocks locking a period with an unexplained variance. (Depends on P0-2 for the GL cash side.)

P1-2 No segregation of duties / dual-approval on disbursements MISSING

Best practice: No single person authorizes, disburses, reconciles, and records. Dual signatures/approvals on payments over a threshold; reserve transfers need two approvers.
YCM today: No AP/disbursement/check-run model and no maker-checker approval anywhere in schema. The audit_logs trail records changes but doesn't enforce a second set of eyes.
Risk: This is the #1 vector in HOA embezzlement cases — one treasurer controlling all four duties. A self-managed 18-unit board is exactly the profile.
Source: Hovland Forensic HOA embezzlement; HOAleader 7-step controls checkup; CAI; MACPA fund-safeguarding.
Recommendation: When AP is built, add approval thresholds + a second-approver gate on disbursements and reserve transfers, with the audit log capturing both approvers.

P1-3 No reserve study / funding plan driving reserves MISSING

Best practice: A reserve study (component inventory + funding plan) refreshed at least every 3 years drives the reserve contribution; target 70–100% funded, ~20% of income to reserves.
YCM today: The GL can hold a reserve fund, but there is no reserve-study data model — no components, no % funded, no contribution driver. Reserve funding would be a guess.
Risk: Under-funded reserves → special assessments (like Cherry Hill's driveway) become emergencies instead of planned events; lender/insurer scrutiny; owner disputes.
Source: CAI National Reserve Study Standards; First Citizens reserve-fund guidance.
Recommendation: Add a reserve-study model (components, useful life, replacement cost, current reserves, % funded) that computes the recommended monthly reserve contribution and shows funded status on the dashboard.

P2-1 Track a special assessment separately from operating dues, by fund PARTIAL

Best practice: A special assessment (e.g. Cherry Hill's driveway) is tracked as its own restricted activity — its own receivable and typically its own fund — not blended into operating dues.
YCM today: special_assessments is a solid model (allocation methods, installments, loan-style terms), and it posts to the ledger — but with GL off there's no fund tag, so the driveway money isn't accounted as separate restricted activity from operating cash.
Source: FASB ASC 972 fund accounting; CAI.
Recommendation: Post special-assessment charges/receipts to a dedicated fund/GL account so the driveway project has its own visible balance and can't be spent on operations. (Rides on P0-2.)

P2-2 No formal AR aging buckets or lien lifecycle PARTIAL

Best practice: Age receivables into current / 30 / 60 / 90+ buckets; track delinquency % against the CAI benchmark (≤3% excellent); model the statutory pre-lien → lien → foreclosure ladder.
YCM today: Strong staged notices (30/60/90) + escalations + collections handoff, but no aging-bucket report and no lien object (notice-of-intent → filed → released).
Source: CAI delinquency benchmarks; state condo lien statutes.
Recommendation: Add an aging report (unit-centric, per P0-1) and a lien lifecycle so the board sees delinquency % and the legal collection state at a glance.

P2-3 No annual audit/review export package MISSING

Best practice: Annual CPA audit or review per state/governing docs, following the AICPA CIRA guide; a clean export package makes it cheap.
YCM today: No audit workflow or CPA export.
Source: AICPA CIRA Audit & Accounting Guide; state statutes.
Recommendation: Once the GL is live, generate a year-end package (trial balance, financials by fund, reserve disclosure, GL detail) for the outside CPA. Lower priority than getting the books right first.
Sequencing note. P0-1 (unit-centric) and P0-3 (unique reference) are the same pivot and should ship together — they make reconciliation deterministic and fix the liability model. P0-2 (turn on the GL) is the unlock for P1-1 (three-way rec), P2-1 (fund-separated special assessments), and P2-3 (audit package). The GL is already built and correct; the work is the expense/vendor side + the reconcile-to-the-cent gate + flipping the flag, not a rewrite.
